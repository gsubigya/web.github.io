GBM (Baseline)
What it assumes:
Returns are normally distributed, volatility is constant, no memory in returns.

NEPSE problem:
NEPSE has volatility clustering (calm then explosive), fat tails, and confirmed AR(1) autocorrelation. GBM ignores all of this.

Use when: You need a fast baseline comparison.
Jump Diffusion (Merton)
What it adds:
Poisson-distributed sudden jumps on top of GBM diffusion. Models NRB regulatory shocks and political crises.

NEPSE calibration:
λ=2 jumps/yr, mean jump −8%, based on 2018, 2022 crash patterns.

Best for: Realistic downside tail modeling.
GARCH-GBM
What it adds:
Volatility is no longer constant it clusters and mean-reverts. NRB 2024 research confirms EGARCH best-fit for NEPSE.

Effect:
After a high-vol period, vol decays toward long-run mean (κ parameter). Produces more realistic path shapes.

Best for: Medium-term (6mo–2yr) volatility forecasting.
Regime Switching
What it adds:
3 hidden states: Bull (μ=25%, σ=18%), Flat (μ=3%, σ=20%), Bear (μ=−20%, σ=35%). Each day the model can transition between states.

NEPSE reality:
2016–2021 bull, 2022 bear, 2022–2024 flat are textbook regime shifts.

Best for: Long-term (2–5yr) multi-cycle projections.

Quick Start Guide
What is Monte Carlo Simulation?
This tool runs thousands of random simulated futures for the NEPSE index, based on historical data and the parameters you set. Each simulation is one possible future. Together, they show the probability distribution of outcomes — helping you understand risk, not predict the future.
1
Choose a Model
Pick one of the 4 simulation models. For most users, Jump Diffusion (Merton) is recommended — it includes sudden shock events like the 2022 NRB policy crash.
2
Set Core Parameters
Set the starting index (default: current NEPSE), number of simulations (more = more accurate, slower), and your time horizon (how far ahead to project).
3
Pick a Scenario or Customize
Use a preset scenario (Bull Run, Bear Market, NRB Shock) or set custom Drift μ and Volatility σ values. Adjust Advanced Parameters for fine-tuning.
4
Run & Read Results
Click Run Simulation. Read the Median (P50), Bull Case (P90), Bear Case (P10), risk metrics, and distribution bar to understand the probability spread.
5
Export Your Data
After running, use the Export buttons to save the chart as PNG, download simulation data as CSV, get full JSON results, or generate an HTML report.
Core Parameters Explained
Starting Index Core
The NEPSE index value from which the simulation begins. Defaults to 2,714.10 (January 2026, CEIC data). You can enter any value useful for backtesting or hypothetical scenarios.
Default: 2,714.10 · Range: 100 – 10,000 · Tip: Use the current live NEPSE value for most accurate projections.
Simulations (N) Core
The number of independent random paths to simulate. More simulations produce smoother, more statistically reliable results. However, higher counts take longer to compute. 2,000 is good for quick exploration; 10,000+ for final analysis.
Default: 2,000 · Range: 200 – 50,000 · Recommended: 5,000–10,000 for reliable stats · Trade-off: Speed vs. accuracy
Time Horizon Core
How many trading days into the future to simulate. NEPSE has approximately 252 trading days per year. The horizon determines how long each simulated path runs.
Default: 252 days (1 year) · Options: 63d (3mo), 126d (6mo), 252d (1yr), 504d (2yr), up to 1,260d (5yr) · Longer horizons show wider uncertainty bands.
Annual Drift μ Core
The expected average annual return of the index, expressed as a percentage. This is the "trend" direction of the simulation. Positive drift means the model expects growth on average; negative drift models a bearish environment. It is derived from the NEPSE CAGR of ~8.69% over 22 years (2003–2025).
Default: 8.69% (historical CAGR) · Bull: 25–35% · Bear: −20% · Crisis: −40% · Range: −40% to +60%
Annual Volatility σ Core
The annualized standard deviation of daily returns — essentially how "wild" the index swings day to day. Higher volatility produces wider path fans and more extreme outcomes in both directions. NEPSE's EGARCH-estimated σ is ~24%, higher than developed markets due to liquidity, political risk, and NRB policy sensitivity.
Default: 24% (EGARCH estimate, NRB 2024) · Normal: 18–28% · Crisis peaks: 45–55% · Range: 8% – 65%
Confidence Interval Core
Controls which percentile bands are drawn on the main chart. A 90% CI shows the P5–P95 band, meaning 90% of simulated paths fall within the shaded region. Wider CIs show more of the tail outcomes.
Options: 80% CI (P10–P90), 90% CI (P5–P95), 95% CI (P2.5–P97.5)
Scenario Preset Core
Quickly loads pre-calibrated μ and σ values based on known NEPSE market regimes. Choose Historical Baseline for the default long-run parameters, or pick scenario-specific presets to model specific environments.
Presets: Historical (μ8.69%, σ24%) · Bull Run (μ35%, σ18%) · Bear Market (μ−20%, σ32%) · NRB Shock (μ−40%, σ50%) · Post-Crisis Recovery (μ28%, σ28%) · Flat/Sideways (μ3%, σ20%)
Advanced Parameters Explained
Fat Tail ν (Student-t degrees of freedom) Advanced
Controls the "fatness" of the tail of the return distribution. Normal distribution (GBM) assumes no extreme outliers — but real markets have far more crashes and rallies than normal distributions predict. The Student-t distribution with low ν (degrees of freedom) produces fatter tails, modeling NEPSE's tendency for sudden extreme moves. Research on NEPSE (arxiv 2026) finds ν≈4–6 to be most appropriate.
Default: 5 · Lower = fatter tails (more extreme events) · 30 ≈ Normal distribution · NEPSE range: 4–6
Jump Intensity λ (jumps per year) Advanced
In the Jump Diffusion model, λ controls how often sudden discontinuous jumps (shocks) occur. A value of 2 means on average 2 jump events happen per year. Each jump is an abrupt price discontinuity — like the NRB loan cap announcement in 2022 which caused a −41.5% drop. Higher λ = more frequent shocks.
Default: 2 · Range: 0 (no jumps / pure GBM) to 10 · NEPSE calibration: ~2/yr based on 2018 & 2022 crises
Mean Jump Size (jμ) Advanced
The average size of each jump event. Negative values model downward shocks (regulatory crackdowns, political crises). Positive values could model surprise positive announcements. This is the mean of the log-normal jump distribution, so the actual price impact is exp(jμ).
Default: −8% · Range: −30% to +10% · NRB shock: −20 to −30% · Positive catalyst: +5 to +10%
Jump Volatility (jσ) Advanced
The standard deviation of each individual jump size. Higher jσ means jump magnitudes are more unpredictable — sometimes small, sometimes catastrophic. Low jσ means jumps are more consistent in their size.
Default: 15% · Range: 5% – 40%
AR(1) Autocorrelation ρ Advanced
Controls how much today's return influences tomorrow's. A positive ρ (momentum) means good days tend to slightly follow good days. A negative ρ (mean-reversion) means big up days are followed by slight pullbacks. ARIMA studies on NEPSE (Gaire, NRB) confirm a small positive ρ ≈ 0.05, consistent with developing market momentum.
Default: 0.05 · Range: −0.20 (strong mean-reversion) to +0.30 (momentum) · 0.00 = no autocorrelation
Seasonality Boost Advanced
When ON, applies monthly drift adjustments based on 20+ years of NEPSE seasonal patterns. The biggest effect is July (+1.4%/month boost, 93% banking win rate due to fiscal year-end). Turning this OFF runs a seasonally-neutral simulation.
Default: ON · July multiplier: +1.4%/mo · August: −0.4%/mo (post-FY correction) · February: −0.2%/mo
GARCH Mean Reversion κ Advanced
Used in the GARCH-GBM model only. Controls how quickly volatility "calms down" after a high-vol period. Higher κ means vol reverts to its long-run average faster. Lower κ means volatile periods persist longer. EGARCH research on NEPSE suggests κ ≈ 0.10–0.20.
Default: 0.15 · Range: 0.01 (slow reversion, vol persists) to 0.50 (fast reversion) · NEPSE estimate: 0.10–0.20
Risk-Free Rate rf Advanced
The annual risk-free rate used to compute the Sharpe and Sortino ratios. In Nepal, this approximates the NRB base rate or short-term treasury bill rate. The Sharpe ratio = (simulated return − rf) / volatility of simulated returns.
Default: 4% (NRB base rate proxy) · Range: 0% – 12% · Use the current NRB policy rate for most accurate Sharpe estimates
Investment Amount (NPR) Advanced
Optional. Enter your investment in Nepali Rupees proportional to an index position. The simulator will calculate P&L projections (Bear, Median, Bull scenarios) in NPR terms after the simulation completes, showing expected profit and loss in absolute rupee values.
Optional · Example: 100,000 NPR · Results show NPR value at P10/P50/P90 index levels · Leave blank to skip
Reading the Results
Chart: Percentile Bands
The main chart shows hundreds of individual simulated paths (colored by outcome: red = loss, green = gain) and overlays percentile bands. The cyan line is the median (P50) — half the simulations ended above this, half below. Green bands show optimistic outcomes; red bands show downside risk.
P10 / P50 / P90 — What They Mean
P10 (Bear Case) = 10% of simulations ended at or below this value. This is your downside scenario to plan for. P50 (Median) = the middle outcome — equally likely to be above or below. P90 (Bull Case) = only 10% of simulations ended above this — this is the optimistic tail.
VaR 95% — Value at Risk
The maximum expected loss with 95% confidence over the simulation period. If VaR 95% = −500 pts, then 95% of simulations ended with a loss no worse than −500 pts. Only 5% of simulations produced worse losses.
CVaR 95% — Conditional Value at Risk (Expected Shortfall)
The average loss of the worst 5% of scenarios. CVaR is stricter than VaR — it tells you what you'd typically lose if you end up in the bad tail. CVaR ≥ VaR always. For risk management, CVaR is the more important metric.
Sharpe Ratio
Measures risk-adjusted return: (annualized return − risk-free rate) ÷ return volatility. Sharpe > 1 = good risk-adjusted performance. Sharpe < 0 = expected return is below the risk-free rate. Use this to compare model configurations, not as a trading signal.
Sortino Ratio
Like Sharpe, but only penalizes downside volatility (losses), not upside volatility. More relevant for asymmetric assets like equities. Higher Sortino = better downside-adjusted return. Sortino > Sharpe means most volatility is upside (positive).
Skewness & Kurtosis
Skewness measures asymmetry of the outcome distribution. Negative skewness = left tail is longer (more extreme losses than gains). Positive = right tail longer (extreme gains possible). Kurtosis measures tail heaviness — excess kurtosis > 0 means more extreme outcomes than a normal distribution would produce.
Max Drawdown (Estimated)
The average of the worst single-path drawdown across all simulations — from peak to trough. Gives a sense of the worst sustained loss within a path, not just the final outcome. Important for investors who cannot tolerate large interim losses even if they recover.
